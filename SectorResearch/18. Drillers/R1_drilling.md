# Earth drilling sector: a global landscape sweep anchored on MDI.TO

**The earth drilling services and equipment sector generates an estimated $175–185 billion annually across oil & gas, mining, geothermal, water, and construction end-markets, growing at a composite 3.5–4.5% CAGR through 2029.** This is a deeply cyclical, capital-intensive sector undergoing rapid consolidation — particularly in offshore drilling, where a 2020 bankruptcy wave has compressed dozens of players into fewer than six scaled competitors. Major Drilling Group International (MDI.TO), the anchor company, sits in the mineral exploration drilling sub-sector (~$3.3B sub-TAM), where it is the world's largest publicly traded specialist with a 700+ rig fleet and C$727M in FY2025 revenue. The broader sector is being reshaped by three forces: platform consolidation via M&A (over **$20B in deals since April 2024**), AI-driven drilling automation creating competitive moats for technology leaders, and a geographic pivot toward the Middle East and deepwater basins as North American rig counts structurally decline.

### Top 5 growth drivers

**1. Offshore deepwater investment renaissance.** Offshore upstream investment grew from **$140B (2021) to ~$250B (2025)**, per Rystad. Brazil (Petrobras $76B E&P capex 2025–2029), Guyana, Namibia, and West Africa are driving deepwater. Rystad projects **60+ offshore frontier wells in 2026**. Offshore segment CAGR of ~8% is the fastest within drilling.

**2. Critical minerals and copper-gold exploration.** Copper exploration exceeded **$3.2B in 2024** (first time above $3B since 2013), per S&P Global. Lithium exploration surpassed **$1B** (up 30% YoY). Uranium exploration rose 33% to $331M. Energy transition metals provide a structural floor to exploration spend even as overall budgets are flat.

**3. Next-generation geothermal drilling.** Fervo Energy has raised **$1.5B+** since founding; its Cape Station project targets 500 MW by 2028. Per-well drilling costs fell from **$9.4M to $4.8M** (49% reduction). The US Inflation Reduction Act provides up to 30% ITC for geothermal through 2034. DOE projects 90+ GW technically achievable in the US alone.

**4. Middle East rig count expansion.** Saudi Aramco's IKTVA program drives localization and capacity investment. ADNOC Drilling's revenue doubled from **$1B (2019) to $2B (2025)**. The ARO joint venture (Valaris/Aramco) has a 50-rig newbuild program. Despite temporary Aramco jackup suspensions in 2024, long-term MENA growth trajectory is intact.

**5. US shale efficiency gains.** Permian production rose **18% (1.0M bpd)** despite a 29% rig count decline from December 2022. Production per rig has roughly doubled since 2014. This is a headwind for rig count but a tailwind for technology providers and super-spec rig operators commanding premium dayrates.

### Top 3 headwinds

**Commodity price cyclicality** dominates. WTI is forecast at ~$51/bbl for 2026 (EIA), down 21% from 2025. OPEC+ is unwinding 2.2M bpd of voluntary cuts. Global upstream investment declining 2–3% in 2026. NA drilling revenue fell **26% from peak** (Q1 2023 to Q4 2025).

**ESG and capital discipline** continue to constrain. Major E&P exploration spending averaged **$7B/year (2020–2024)**, down 30% from $10B/year (2016–2019). Grassroots mineral exploration share fell to an **all-time low of 22%** (2024). Clean energy investment ($2T in 2024) vastly outpaces upstream O&G ($570B).

**Offshore dayrate pressure** is emerging. Westwood reports 2025 jackup dayrates fell **20.6%** and drillship dayrates fell **8.2%**. Saudi Aramco's suspension of 36+ jackups flooded the global market. Global marketed committed utilization declined from **94% (2023) to 89% (2025)**. An aging fleet (only 24% younger than 15 years by 2028) will require an estimated $66B in replacement investment.

## 5. Platform consolidation dynamics

### Offshore: from bankruptcy wave to oligopoly

The offshore drilling sector underwent the most dramatic restructuring in energy services history. The oil price collapse of 2014–2016 ($107 to $26/bbl) and COVID-19 in 2020 forced **Diamond Offshore, Noble Corp, Valaris, Pacific Drilling, and Seadrill** into Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Billions in debt were equitized or eliminated. Post-emergence, a rapid consolidation arc followed: Noble acquired Pacific Drilling (2021), then Maersk Drilling (2022, ~$3.4B), then Diamond Offshore (2024, $1.6B). Seadrill emerged from bankruptcy in February 2022 and exited jackups entirely. ADES absorbed Shelf Drilling. The pending **Transocean-Valaris merger ($5.8B, announced February 2026)** would create a 73-rig behemoth with $10B+ backlog.

By late 2026, the offshore sector will likely consist of **3–4 major players**: a mega Transocean (if the Valaris deal closes, plus potential Seadrill), Noble (floater-focused), ADES (jackup-dominant), and Borr (pure jackup). Companies are specializing — Noble and Transocean are pivoting to deepwater-only, divesting jackups. ADES and Borr are absorbing the jackup segment.

### US land: vertical integration as the new playbook

**Patterson-UTI/NexTier ($5.4B, September 2023)** was the landmark deal, combining PTEN's #2 drilling fleet with NexTier's leading frac fleet to offer operators spud-to-production service. H&P/KCA Deutag ($1.97B) was geographic expansion rather than vertical integration, though H&P continues expanding its AutoSlide/FlexFusion technology platform. Nabors/Parker Wellbore ($472M) represents horizontal expansion within the drilling value chain. With rig count plateaued at ~526, growth comes from **increasing revenue per rig** through integrated offerings and automation, not fleet expansion.

### Integrated OFS platforms absorb selectively

SLB's ChampionX acquisition ($7.8B) extends it into production chemicals, artificial lift, and emissions tech — building an end-to-end reservoir lifecycle platform. Baker Hughes is moving in the **opposite direction**: activist Ananym Capital (November 2025) pushed for OFSE spin-off, arguing OFSE (6–7x EBITDA) drags on IET valuation (16–18x EBITDA). **Functions being absorbed**: production chemicals, artificial lift, digital reservoir management. **Functions staying independent**: pure contract drilling, commodity pressure pumping, consumables.

### Mineral exploration drilling: fragmented but consolidation beginning

This sub-sector remains fragmented because of low barriers at small scale, relationship-driven customer dynamics, cyclical demand, geographic dispersion, and founder/family ownership. However, consolidation is accelerating because mining companies increasingly want larger, safer, technologically capable partners. Major Drilling (MDI.TO) has executed a disciplined acquisition strategy — McKay (Australia, 2021, A$80M), Explomin (Peru, 2024, $85M) — targeting specialty/high-barrier markets and senior mining company relationships. Perenti's DDH1 acquisition ($274M, 2023) created a 290-rig fleet. **Scale enables technology investment (automation, remote operation) that smaller operators cannot match.**

Baker Hughes rig count data provides the definitive record of the sector's brutal cyclicality:

| Cycle | Peak | Trough | Decline | Recovery time |
|-------|------|--------|---------|---------------|
| 2008–2009 GFC | ~2,031 US rigs (Aug 2008) | ~876 (Jun 2009) | **57%** | ~2 years |
| 2014–2016 oil crash | ~1,931 (Oct 2014) | 404 (May 2016) | **79%** | ~4 years (partial) |
| 2020 COVID | ~796 (Jan 2020) | **244** (Aug 2020 — all-time record low) | **69%** | ~2 years to 750 peak |
| 2022–present | 750 (Dec 2022) | ~526 (Aug 2025) | 30% | Ongoing |

Peak-to-trough declines of **57–79%** are typical. Over **200 energy companies** filed for bankruptcy during 2015–2017, per Haynes & Boone. The 2020 COVID trough of 244 US rigs was the lowest since at least the 1940s. The international rig count hit its lowest since 1975 at 1,016 rigs in October 2020. This is a sector where capital gets destroyed rapidly in downturns and reallocated rapidly in recoveries.

### AI impact: net tailwind, with nuances

**Drilling optimization and automation (TAILWIND).** SLB's PRECISE™ Rig Operating System and Neuro™ autonomous directional drilling replace human directional drillers with AI-driven systems. In January 2024, SLB and Nabors announced a landmark collaboration to cross-integrate their platforms (PRECISE™ + SmartROS®). H&P's FlexFusion® enables "one-button" drilling — entire stands drilled without human intervention. AutoSlide™ provides autonomous slide drilling using machine learning, saving ~$2,000/day per directional driller replaced while delivering more consistent wellbore quality. H&P describes its automation at "Level 4" (driver present but not steering).

**AI-driven geological modeling (MILD HEADWIND).** Machine learning models can predict reservoir characteristics from existing data, reducing exploration drilling volume. However, this headwind is mild because development drilling (the bulk of activity) is less affected, and better subsurface understanding tends to unlock more resources, not fewer.

**Autonomous rigs (TAILWIND for equipment OEMs).** Nabors and H&P are developing the most advanced onshore autonomous rig capabilities. Epiroc offers fully autonomous surface drill rigs (Pit Viper, SmartROC) for mining. Rig floor automation (robotic pipe handling, automated iron roughnecks) is becoming standard on premium rigs.

**Net assessment: TAILWIND.** AI and automation create margin enhancement, competitive differentiation for technology leaders, new demand through economic development of marginal wells, and a capex upgrade cycle. The mild exploration volume headwind is overwhelmed by these positives. The SLB-Nabors cross-platform collaboration signals the industry is entering open-architecture drilling automation, accelerating adoption.
