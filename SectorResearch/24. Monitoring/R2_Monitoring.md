# Cloud observability sector: six-company standardized metrics

**Datadog dominates this peer group on growth and scale, while PagerDuty's sub-100% NRR and SolarWinds' delisting reshape the competitive landscape.** Across the six Cloud Observability / Monitoring names analyzed — DDOG, DT, ESTC, PD, SWI, and NTCT — growth rates span from negative (NTCT at −0.5% quarterly YoY) to nearly 30% (DDOG), and business models range from pure SaaS to hardware-heavy hybrids. SolarWinds is no longer publicly traded (acquired by Turn/River Capital, closed April 2025), and NetScout operates a fundamentally different model where standard SaaS metrics do not apply. This report pulls from the most recent official earnings releases and SEC filings for each company, with every data point flagged for confidence.

## What the numbers reveal across the group

**Datadog stands alone on growth quality.** Its **29% quarterly revenue growth**, **120% NRR**, and **52% RPO growth** all rank first. Its Rule of 40 score of ~55 comfortably exceeds the threshold despite investing heavily. The trade-off is valuation: at **9.2x EV/NTM revenue**, DDOG trades at more than double any peer. SBC at **21.9% of revenue** is the highest in the group — a dilution risk worth monitoring even as absolute SBC growth decelerates.

**Dynatrace and Elastic occupy the middle tier.** Both grew revenue ~18% YoY in the most recent quarter with similar NRR profiles (111–112%). Dynatrace edges ahead on profitability (**29.4% non-GAAP operating margin** vs. Elastic's 16.6%) and FCF generation (24% vs. 15.3%). Dynatrace carries **zero debt** and $1.2B in cash. Elastic's RPO growth of 22% and Cloud revenue growth of 21% suggest its transition to cloud-native delivery is tracking well. Both companies raised guidance, signaling execution confidence.

**PagerDuty's metrics flash warning signals.** A **98% DBNRR** means the existing customer base is contracting in dollar terms — a rare and concerning state for a SaaS company. ARR growth of just **1%**, combined with flat FY2027 guidance ($492.5M vs. $492.5M actual FY2026), suggests the company has hit a growth ceiling in its current form. The saving grace is margin expansion: non-GAAP operating margin reached 24.6% and FCF margin 20.9%, with SBC declining sharply. At **0.94x EV/NTM revenue** and **4.1x EV/NTM FCF**, the stock is priced as a value/restructuring play, not a growth SaaS name.

**SolarWinds is no longer observable.** Taken private by Turn/River Capital in April 2025 at a ~6.7x EV/LTM revenue multiple, SolarWinds' last public data showed a classic subscription transition: **34% subscription ARR growth** masked by maintenance decline (−4.6%) and license erosion (−17%), producing just 5% total revenue growth. The 29-percentage-point divergence between subscription ARR growth and total revenue growth was the largest transition gap in the group. Under private ownership, the transition will continue without public disclosure.

**NetScout is the outlier by design.** As a hardware-plus-services company focused on network visibility and DDoS protection, it lacks SaaS metrics entirely. Revenue was essentially flat in Q3 (−0.5% YoY) though the nine-month figure grew 6.3%. The company is highly profitable (non-GAAP operating margin ~25.8%) with **$586M in net cash and zero debt**. Its **7.1% SBC-to-revenue ratio** (tied with SWI for lowest) and **~1.8x EV/NTM revenue** reflect its mature, capital-efficient profile. Service revenue (56% of TTM) is gradually overtaking product revenue.

---

## Conclusion

This peer group spans the full SaaS lifecycle — from hypergrowth (DDOG) through steady compounding (DT, ESTC), into stagnation (PD), privatization (SWI), and legacy hardware/services (NTCT). The key differentiators for sector analysis are RPO growth and NRR as forward-looking health indicators. DDOG's 52% RPO growth and 120% NRR signal expanding wallet share and lengthening contracts, while PD's sub-100% NRR and 2% RPO growth indicate structural headwinds. For valuation screening, DT and ESTC offer the most balanced risk/reward profiles: solid double-digit growth, expanding profitability, raised guidance, and moderate multiples (4–5x and 2–3x EV/NTM revenue respectively). Any model incorporating SWI should treat it as a private comp with stale data and significant transition noise.
