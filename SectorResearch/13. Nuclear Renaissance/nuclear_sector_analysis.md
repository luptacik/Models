## SECTION 3: NARRATIVE REPORT

### 3a. Sub-sector Dynamics

The nuclear renaissance is real, but it is uneven. The next 2–3 years of investable value creation are concentrated in three sub-sectors: uranium supply chain tightening, nuclear services backlog conversion, and existing fleet monetization via data center PPAs.

**Uranium mining** is the most structurally bullish. Mine supply (~63 ktU) continues to fall short of reactor demand (~69 ktU). Utilities are under-contracted for the 13th consecutive year, covering only ~50% of replacement rate in 2025. Cameco disclosed that 70% of new 2025 contracts were market-related with ceilings up to $150/lb — the publicly reported $88 spot price significantly understates the true clearing price for committed supply. The Russia uranium import ban (full effect from Jan 2028) removes ~40% of global enrichment capacity from Western markets. This is not a cyclical upturn — it is a structural supply deficit widening on the demand side (China targeting 110 GW by 2030, Japan restarts, new Western builds) while supply response lags by 5–10 years from price signal to new mine production.

**Nuclear services and components** are experiencing the strongest order intake in 40+ years. BWXT backlog +50% YoY to $7.3B. AtkinsRéalis nuclear revenue +53% organic to C$2.3B. Mirion backlog +36% to $1.1B. Doosan nuclear orders +77% YoY. Westinghouse signed an $80B partnership with the US government. These are not speculative commitments — they represent contracted engineering, manufacturing, and construction work flowing through P&Ls over the next 3–7 years. The backlog wave confirms the nuclear buildout is transitioning from policy aspiration to contracted reality.

**Existing nuclear fleet operators** (CEG, VST) are monetizing decades-old assets at prices nobody imagined. Nuclear PPAs are being signed at $100–130/MWh for 20-year terms — 2–3× wholesale power prices — because hyperscalers need 24/7 carbon-free baseload and only nuclear delivers it. Meta's 6.6 GW of nuclear PPAs (Jan 2026) is the largest corporate nuclear procurement in history. The addressable opportunity is enormous: only ~25% of CEG's clean firm output is contracted.

**SMR developers** are the sector's most dangerous area for capital allocation. The divergence is stark: NuScale (only NRC certification, down 80% from peak), OKLO (Meta 1.2 GW deal, down 74% from peak), and NNE ($0 revenue, $577M cash) collectively represent billions in market cap resting on technology that won't generate meaningful revenue until the 2030s. No Western SMR is in commercial operation. China's HTR-PM and Russia's KLT-40S are the only operating SMRs globally. TerraPower's construction permit (March 2026) is the most significant Western milestone, but first units aren't expected until 2030–2031. The gap between hyperscaler demand (NOW) and SMR supply (mid-2030s) is being filled by natural gas — creating tension with clean energy commitments.

### 3b. Competitive Landscape

**Who is gaining share:**
- **Cameco/Westinghouse** is consolidating the Western nuclear supply chain via vertical integration. The $80B government partnership positions Westinghouse as the preferred AP1000 OEM for US, Poland, Ukraine, and allied new builds. Cameco's market-related contract structure means revenue ratchets higher as uranium prices climb.
- **AtkinsRéalis** is winning nuclear services contracts at an accelerating pace — 53% organic growth is extraordinary for an engineering firm. Darlington SMR, Pickering refurbishment, Bruce Power MSA, and CANDU international services create a multi-decade pipeline.
- **BWXT** is expanding from primarily naval nuclear into commercial nuclear services via acquisitions (Kinectrics, A.O.T.) and TRISO fuel production. Commercial segment +63% growth validates the diversification.
- **GEV** is gaining in gas turbines (primary business) and positioned for the SMR market via BWRX-300, with construction underway at OPG Darlington and 24 units planned for Poland.

**Who is losing share or struggling:**
- **NuScale** is the clearest loser: UAMPS cancellation, securities fraud class action, Fluor monetizing stake, revenue declining. The NRC certification is valuable IP but execution has eroded confidence.
- **Kazatomprom** faces MET hike, acid supply issues, and production constraints that are allowing Western miners to gain relative positioning. Still dominant on volume but cost structure is deteriorating.
- **Energy Fuels (UUUU)** is trying to diversify into REE but revenue is declining while costs and dilution escalate. The White Mesa Mill is a unique asset but the company is not generating returns from it.

**Key head-to-head battles:**
- **CCJ vs KAP:** The two dominant miners. CCJ's vertical integration (Westinghouse) and Western geopolitical alignment give it a structural premium. KAP's lower costs and higher margins offset by government ownership and MET risks. Market awards CCJ ~26x EV/EBITDA vs KAP ~18x — a justified gap.
- **BWXT vs CW:** Both serve nuclear equipment/defense. BWXT has the naval monopoly and higher growth (+18% vs +12%); CW has the better business model (15.8% FCF margin, 1.3x leverage vs 9.2% and 3.5x). CW is the better-run company; BWXT has the more defensible moat.
- **OKLO vs NuScale vs GEV (BWRX-300):** The SMR race. NuScale has the only NRC certification but cratered execution. OKLO has the biggest commercial deal (Meta 1.2 GW) but earliest technology. GEV has the first unit under construction and the backing of the world's largest power equipment company. GEV is the most likely to reach commercial deployment first via BWRX-300, which is a proven BWR design in a smaller package — lower technology risk than OKLO's fast reactor or NuScale's modular approach.

**M&A outlook:**
- **Likely acquirors:** Cameco/Brookfield/Westinghouse (uranium juniors, enrichment stakes), Constellation Energy (nuclear plants, gas assets), BWXT (nuclear services companies), Brookfield (infrastructure assets).
- **Likely targets:** NuScale (NRC-certified SMR IP at $2.4B EV, distressed — strategic acquiror at 3–5x current could capture massive option value), LEU (national security asset — potential government-supported transaction), small uranium juniors (Athabasca Basin consolidation continuing), Mirion (installed-base moat at 18.8x EBITDA — attractive to defense prime or industrial platform).
- **M&A pricing:** Nuclear plants transact at 7–10x EBITDA (Energy Harbor at 8x is the comp). Nuclear services at 12–18x EBITDA. Uranium in-ground at $3–8/lb of resource.

### 3e. Conviction Summary

This is a sector where the fundamental tailwinds — AI data center demand, de-Russification of the nuclear supply chain, bipartisan government support, structural uranium supply deficit — are as strong as any I have seen across the sectors analyzed to date. Morgan Stanley projects $2.2T in cumulative nuclear investment through 2050. The data center demand alone (>13 GW of hyperscaler nuclear PPAs signed) would have been inconceivable three years ago. The tailwinds are real. The question is where to position.

My highest-conviction candidates are the three names at 22/25: **CCJ, CEG, and ATRL**. Each represents a different expression of the nuclear renaissance thesis with distinct risk profiles. CCJ is the vertically integrated uranium-to-reactor-OEM platform — the most complete nuclear infrastructure business in the Western world, with a contract book that will ratchet higher for years as market-related pricing escalates. CEG is the fleet monetization play — 22 GW of licensed nuclear capacity that took 30+ years to build, now being sold to hyperscalers at premium prices, trading at the cheapest valuation in the sector after a 27% YTD decline. ATRL is the mispriced services pick — record fundamentals, nuclear supercycle positioning, net cash balance sheet, and yet the stock sits 25% below consensus with zero sell ratings.

Behind these three, **KAP** offers the cheapest entry to uranium mining economics (18x EV/EBITDA vs CCJ at 26x) with the highest margins in the industry (63% EBITDA), though Kazakhstan risks keep it below CCJ in conviction. **BWXT** is a defense-grade monopoly with extraordinary backlog growth but carries leverage and a premium multiple. **CW** is the best-run business in the group on pure financial quality but is a diversified industrial with limited nuclear purity.

What would change my mind: a uranium price correction below $60/lb sustained for 6+ months would fundamentally alter the miner economics and undermine the supply-deficit thesis. A major Western construction failure (cost overruns on BWRX-300, Natrium, or a new AP1000 project) would damage the nuclear renaissance narrative. And if AI data center power demand growth disappoints materially (hyperscalers scaling back capex), the nuclear PPA pipeline would compress, hitting CEG and the equipment companies hardest.
