**Competitive Moat (5s):** GEV — gas turbine oligopoly (3 major OEMs globally), $150B backlog, sold out through 2030. CCJ — McArthur River (world's richest uranium deposit, 23% ore grade), 230M lbs under long-term contract. LEU — sole licensed US HALEU producer, national security asset. These are genuine physical-asset moats that cannot be replicated on any relevant timeline.

**Business Model Quality (1s):** OKLO and SMR are pre-revenue with significant cash burn. Per calibration guidance, pre-revenue companies are capped at 2 maximum on this dimension — and both score 1 due to no binding commercial contracts and no clear near-term positive EBITDA path.

GEV at implied ~20x+ forward EBITDA and FIX/STRL at 25-45x PE are paying premium multiples for extraordinary backlog visibility — scored 2 (premium requiring execution).

**Automatic Disqualification:** OKLO scores 1 on Competitive Moat (pre-revenue with unproven technology, no binding commercial contracts — all ~18 GW of pipeline is non-binding) and 1 on Business Model Quality (zero revenue, $80-100M annual cash burn). ## SECTION 3: NARRATIVE REPORT

### 3a. Sub-Sector Dynamics

The data center power sector is experiencing a structural demand inflection unprecedented in the history of the power industry. Global DC power demand is projected to roughly double from ~415 TWh (2024) to ~945 TWh by 2030, driven by AI workloads that consume 10x the power of traditional compute per rack. NVIDIA's Vera Rubin NVL144 racks are targeting ~600 kW per rack — a 40x increase from standard 15 kW servers. This creates cascading demand across the entire power value chain.

**Winning sub-sectors (next 2-3 years):**

Data center power and thermal infrastructure (VRT, ETN, MOD, AAON) is the highest-growth, highest-margin sub-sector. Liquid cooling demand roughly doubled in 2025 and is growing 20-40% annually, with air cooling failing above ~15 kW/rack. The M&A frenzy in liquid cooling (Boyd→Eaton at 22.5x EBITDA, CoolIT→Ecolab at 29x NTM EBITDA) confirms the market is paying 2-3x typical industrial valuations for scarce cooling assets.

Electrical/mechanical contractors (FIX, STRL, EME, IESC) are the immediate beneficiaries of hyperscaler buildout. Data center work now represents 40-70% of revenue for these companies, with backlogs doubling YoY across the group. This sub-sector has the strongest near-term revenue visibility because backlog conversion is 12-18 months — orders placed today become revenue in 2026-2027.

Gas turbine/grid equipment (GEV, Siemens Energy, ETN switchgear, POWL) represents the critical supply-constrained bottleneck. Transformer lead times exceed 128 weeks, GEV's gas turbines are sold out through 2030, and grid interconnection queues hold 2,600 GW of backlog. Companies with manufacturing capacity have extraordinary pricing power — GEV pricing at ~$2,500/kW for US gas turbine orders.

Nuclear operators (CEG, TLN, VST) represent the long-term structural play. Nuclear is the only source of 24/7 carbon-free baseload power at scale, and hyperscalers have demonstrated willingness to pay 2-3x wholesale prices for nuclear PPAs (CEG's Microsoft TMI deal at ~$100-130/MWh). But new nuclear is 5-15 years away from commercial deployment, making existing fleets the scarce asset.

**Losing/challenged sub-sectors:**

SMR developers (OKLO, NuScale) remain pre-revenue concept plays. No commercial SMR has operated in the US. Regulatory timelines are 5-10 years, and the HALEU fuel supply chain (LEU is the only US producer) creates an additional bottleneck. The market narrative has run far ahead of commercial reality.

Pure renewable for DC baseload (solar-only, wind) scores 2 for DC tailwind per the calibration file. Intermittency makes these supplementary, not primary, solutions for 24/7 hyperscaler demand. Battery storage helps but cannot bridge overnight gaps at the scale required.

### 3b. Competitive Landscape

**Share gainers (with evidence):**

VRT: Backlog grew from ~$7B to $15B in 12 months. NVIDIA reference architecture partnership means VRT is being specified into designs before competing bids are even considered. Liquid cooling revenue doubled in Q1 2025.

GEV: Gas power equipment backlog surged from 62 GW to 83 GW. FY2025 orders of $59.3B (+34% organic) exceed revenue of $38.1B by 55% — the growth pipeline is accelerating, not decelerating.

FIX: Technology sector (data centers) rose from 33% to 45% of revenue in one year, while total revenue grew 29%. FIX is not just riding the wave — it is gaining share within DC construction.

CEG: Calpine acquisition created a 55 GW national platform. Signed PPAs with Microsoft, Meta, and CyrusOne that collectively represent billions in contracted revenue over 10-20 years. The "one-stop shop for the global data economy" positioning is being validated by contract wins.

**Key head-to-head battles:**

VRT vs. Schneider Electric vs. Eaton: The battle for "platform" status in DC power and cooling equipment. Eaton's $9.5B Boyd acquisition created "chip-to-grid"; Schneider has "grid-to-chip" via EcoStruxure + Motivair; VRT leads via NVIDIA reference architecture. All three are winning — the market is large enough — but specification wins at the design stage will determine long-term share.

CEG vs. TLN vs. VST: Nuclear-to-DC PPA competition. CEG leads on scale (55 GW), TLN has the most valuable single contract (AWS Susquehanna), and VST is the cheapest on EV/EBITDA. CEG's Calpine deal gives it gas + nuclear + geothermal flexibility that TLN and VST cannot match.

FIX vs. STRL vs. EME: DC mechanical/electrical contracting. FIX leads on margin quality (25.5% gross margin, record) and backlog scale ($11.94B). STRL is the fastest-growing (+123% E-Infra Q4 revenue) but smallest. EME has the broadest service offering but guided to flat-to-down EPS in 2026.

**M&A outlook:**

Likely acquirors: Eaton (most aggressive — $12.3B+ deployed), Vertiv (selective gap-filling), Schneider (completing "grid-to-chip"), Trane Technologies (assembling end-to-end cooling).

Likely targets: MOD (subscale cooling, logical for VRT/Schneider at ~$10B), NVT (DC power/cooling bolt-on for larger platform), AAON (BASX brand is attractive if margins recover), POWL (scarce switchgear capacity — worth 20-25x EBITDA to a strategic buyer), BE (strategic for utility/hyperscaler integration).

M&A valuation benchmarks: Liquid cooling assets transacting at 22-29x EBITDA. Nuclear operators at 7-10x EBITDA (CEG premium at 12-18x reflects DC PPA optionality). Electrical equipment at 18-25x EBITDA.

### 3c. Risk Register

**SECTOR-LEVEL RISKS**

**1. Hyperscaler capex pullback (SEVERITY: HIGH)**
AI services revenue of ~$25B in 2025 is only ~10% of cumulative infrastructure spending. If AI monetization disappoints over the next 2-3 years, hyperscalers could slow or freeze capex programs. This is the single scenario that would simultaneously crush every company in this sector. Mitigant: competitive dynamics make pullback "catastrophic" for any individual hyperscaler — stopping building means losing GPU access and training capability.

**2. Grid interconnection and permitting delays (SEVERITY: HIGH)**
US interconnection queue holds 2,600 GW with median time from request to operation of ~5 years. Only 13% of capacity entering queues 2000-2019 reached commercial operation. $64B in DC projects have been blocked or delayed by community opposition. 300+ state data center bills filed across 30+ states in early 2026. This risk constrains the speed at which demand translates to actual construction — benefiting behind-the-meter solutions (BE, SEI) but limiting grid-connected expansion.

**3. Equipment supply chain constraints (SEVERITY: MEDIUM-HIGH)**
Transformer lead times exceed 128 weeks with a 30% supply shortfall. Gas turbine lead times of 5-7 years for heavy-duty units. Supply growing 3-4% vs. 7-9% demand growth. This is a double-edged risk: it limits the sector's growth rate but creates pricing power for companies with manufacturing capacity (GEV, ETN, POWL).

**4. Interest rate environment (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)**
Capital-intensive power infrastructure is directly rate-sensitive. A -200bps rate cut improves project NPV by 15-22%. Conversely, higher-for-longer rates compress PPA economics and increase financing costs for new construction. Monitor each FOMC cycle — direct impact on CEG, TLN, VST, NEE project economics.

**5. Tariff and trade policy uncertainty (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)**
Benefits domestic manufacturers (POWL, ETN, FSLR, GNRC) but pressures import-dependent supply chains. NVT flagged ~$80M tariff impact; MNTR.ST saw margin contraction; FSLR idled SE Asian capacity. Net effect is likely positive for US-focused names in this universe but creates margin uncertainty.

**COMPANY-SPECIFIC RISKS**

- **VRT:** Manufacturing capacity execution risk — Ironton liquid cooling expansion must deliver on time (Q2 2027) to meet backlog conversion
- **GEV:** Wind segment breakeven still unproven; simultaneous multi-segment scaling risk
- **CEG:** Crane Clean Energy Center grid connection delay (PJM transmission upgrades not until 2031); 4.4 GW PJM generation divestiture required for merger approval
- **FIX:** Customer concentration in hyperscaler-adjacent projects; labor availability in tight construction market
- **TLN:** Leverage at ~3.0x post-acquisitions; Susquehanna single-plant concentration risk
- **BE:** Analyst consensus divided (12 Buy / 9 Hold / 4 Sell); stock trading above median PT — potential trap if execution falters
- **SEI:** xAI = 67% of orderbook — single-customer concentration is a material risk
- **IESC:** 84 insider sales / 0 purchases by Executive Chairman — requires explanation
- **CCJ:** Uranium price decline to <$60/lb would compress margins and P/NAV

### 3e. Conviction Summary

This is the strongest demand environment the power generation and data center infrastructure sector has experienced in at least a generation, and possibly ever. Record backlogs are near-universal: VRT $15B, ETN $19.6B, GEV $150B, FIX $12B, EME $13.3B, Schneider €25.4B, Siemens Energy €138B. Every major equipment company and E&C contractor is operating at or near capacity. The binding constraint has shifted from demand to supply — transformer lead times of 128+ weeks, gas turbine sold out through 2030, and grid interconnection queues of 2,600 GW.

The highest-conviction names for downstream valuation work are VRT (22/25), GEV (21/25), CCJ (21/25), FIX (20/25), ETN (20/25), CEG (20/25), and TLN (20/25). These companies share three characteristics: they control scarce physical assets or manufacturing capacity that cannot be replicated on relevant timescales; they have multi-year contracted revenue or backlog visibility; and their management teams have demonstrated execution discipline through beat-and-raise cycles. VRT stands out for combining the highest growth rate with strong profitability and a clean balance sheet. GEV stands out for the largest absolute backlog with an extraordinary margin expansion trajectory. CCJ stands out as the most defensible business with decades of contract coverage.

The primary development that would change this thesis is a sustained hyperscaler capex pullback driven by AI monetization disappointment. If AI services revenue fails to scale from its current ~$25B toward $100B+ by 2028, the competitive pressure that currently prevents any single hyperscaler from reducing capex would dissipate, and the $600B+ annual spending cycle would decelerate. The backlogs provide 2-5 years of revenue protection depending on the company, but stock multiples — many of which price in 2027-2028 growth — would compress immediately. The secondary risk is the rate environment: this is a capital-intensive sector where a 200bps rate increase would materially impair project economics across nuclear restart, new gas generation, and grid expansion.
