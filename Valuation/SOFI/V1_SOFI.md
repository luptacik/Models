# SoFi Technologies: R1 Company Research Dossier

**SoFi Technologies Inc. (NASDAQ: SOFI) | Consumer Fintech / Neobanking**
**Fiscal Year End: December 31 | Reporting: US GAAP | Dossier Date: April 4, 2026**
**Sector Score: 19/25 (PASS) | Rule of 40: 68 | 9 Consecutive GAAP-Profitable Quarters**

SoFi is the only US fintech that owns the full stack — a national bank charter, a B2B technology platform (Galileo/Technisys powering 128M+ accounts), and a consumer super-app with **13.7 million members**. FY2025 GAAP net revenue reached **$3.61 billion** (+35% YoY) with **$481 million in GAAP net income**, and management guided FY2026 to **$4.66 billion** in adjusted revenue (+30%) with **$0.60 adjusted EPS**. The stock trades at **~$15–19** (down ~35–50% YTD), pressured by the Muddy Waters short report (March 17, 2026), two dilutive equity raises totaling $3 billion, and a broader growth-to-value rotation. CEO Noto purchased **$1.5 million** in open-market shares in March 2026 — including on the day the short report dropped — while **zero insider open-market sales** have occurred in five years.

**Critical accounting note**: SoFi carries **96%** of its loan book at fair value (personal + student + home = $36.4B). ## Module 3: Cash flow deep dive

### Why GAAP CFO is misleading for SoFi

SoFi is a bank holding company. GAAP CFO includes **loan origination outflows** ($36.4B in FY2025) offset by loan sale inflows, deposit changes, and warehouse funding. This makes reported CFO deeply negative and extremely volatile — it does not reflect the operating cash generation of the business. Per CEO Noto: *"We believe EBITDA less CapEx is a better measurement of cash flow."*

## Module 9: Competitive positioning

### SoFi's unique own-the-stack architecture

SoFi is the **only US company** that simultaneously owns a national bank charter, a B2B fintech infrastructure platform, and a consumer financial super-app. This vertical integration creates three distinct competitive advantages:

The **bank charter** (obtained January 2022) provides FDIC-insured deposit funding at ~3.34%, roughly **190 bps cheaper** than warehouse facilities — generating an estimated **$627M** in annualized funding cost savings. It also enables direct stablecoin issuance (SoFiUSD) and eliminates the "sponsor bank" dependency that creates regulatory risk for uncharterd competitors like Chime and Cash App.

**Galileo/Technisys** powers **128M+** enabled accounts globally, functioning as the infrastructure layer for competitors who must pay SoFi for card issuing, payment processing, and core banking technology. This creates a paradox where SoFi profits from competitors' growth. Against Marqeta (which derives ~70% of revenue from Block/Cash App), Galileo offers broader capabilities (core banking + card issuing + payments vs. card-only) and a more diversified client base.

The **consumer super-app** leverages the 40% cross-buy rate to drive near-zero marginal customer acquisition costs on second and third products, powering the ARPU expansion from $65/product (FY2024) to $104/product (Q4 2025 annualized).

### Competitive landscape

SoFi's **13.7M members** and **$37.5B in deposits** position it as a top-5 US neobank. Chime (~38M accounts) is larger by users but lacks a bank charter and lending products. Cash App (~55M MAUs) is payments-first with limited banking. Ally Financial (~11M customers) is a full-service digital bank but lacks the technology platform moat. The biggest competitive threat comes from **JPMorgan Chase**, which has unlimited resources and 80M+ digital users — but legacy technology stacks and branch overhead create structural cost disadvantages against SoFi's born-digital model.

### Deposit competitiveness

SoFi offers **3.30% APY** savings (with direct deposit), down from 4.60% peaks in 2023–2024 but still vastly above the national FDIC average of ~0.42%. The gradual rate reduction demonstrates early pricing power as the deposit base becomes stickier through direct deposit relationships and multi-product engagement.

### Muddy Waters short report: serious allegations, muted market response

On March 17, 2026, Muddy Waters published a 28-page report alleging that SoFi's 2025 Adjusted EBITDA was inflated by ~90% (claiming "true" EBITDA of ~$103M vs. reported $1.054B), that $312M in debt was unrecorded, and that the personal loan charge-off rate was actually 6.1% vs. SoFi's reported 2.89%. The report described SoFi's structures as "Enron-esque" and "GE Capital-style."

SoFi responded the same day, calling the report "factually inaccurate and misleading" and exploring potential defamation action. However, SoFi did **not** issue a point-by-point rebuttal, which Muddy Waters criticized in a March 21 follow-up. **Mizuho's Dan Dolev** acknowledged the report's detail but concluded it "misunderstands or mischaracterizes key facts" — specifically, that Muddy Waters used inappropriate discount rates for student loan valuation and misunderstood fair value accounting conventions. **KBW** and other analysts maintained constructive views. Fortune characterized the report as "a misfire" based on the stock's muted reaction.

The stock dipped briefly but has traded roughly in line with the S&P 500 since publication. The **CEO's same-day $500K open-market purchase** was the most powerful counter-signal. The core risk is that SoFi has not addressed specific allegations in detail — the Q1 2026 earnings call (April 29) will be the key test of management's willingness to engage with the accounting questions.

## Conclusion: what this dossier tells a valuation analyst

Three structural realities define SoFi's investment case and should anchor any valuation model. First, the **revenue quality transformation** is real and accelerating — fee-based revenue grew 59% to ~$1.55B in FY2025, reaching 43% of total revenue, and the Loan Platform Business alone generated $576M in fees by originating-to-distribute $11.3B in loans without retaining credit risk. This capital-light revenue stream deserves a meaningfully higher multiple than balance-sheet lending.

Second, the **dilution tax** is the key investor pain point. Shares outstanding grew **16%** in FY2025 through $3B in equity raises plus SBC. GAAP EPS was flat at $0.39 despite net income growing from ($301M) to $481M over two years because the share count increased 34%. Any DCF or LBO model must sensitize dilution assumptions — the convertible 2026 Notes ($530M, maturing October 2026) add near-term refinancing risk and potential further dilution.

Third, the **Muddy Waters overhang** creates a quantifiable optionality for buy-the-dip positioning. The allegations center on fair value accounting conventions that are standard for bank holding companies but poorly understood by generalist investors. The stock's muted post-report reaction, analyst pushback from Mizuho and KBW, and the CEO's same-day $500K purchase all suggest the market assigns low probability to material restatement risk. However, the absence of a detailed rebuttal means this risk is not fully resolved — **April 29 earnings** is the clearing event.

For sum-of-parts modeling, apply differentiated multiples: **Lending** (~$1.85B revenue, 2.80% NCO, 5.72% NIM) merits a bank P/TBV or low-single-digit EV/Revenue; **Financial Services** (~$1.54B, growing 88%, capital-light fees) merits a growth-adjusted EV/Revenue of 5–8x; **Technology Platform** (~$450M, 39% contribution margin, recurring B2B) merits a SaaS-like 6–10x EV/Revenue. The consolidated TBV of **$7.01/share** provides a floor, while the FY2026 consensus revenue of $4.77B at a growth-adjusted 3–4x EV/Revenue implies $11–15B enterprise value. At 1.35B fully diluted shares, this translates to a $8–11/share equity value at the low end or $18–25 at more generous multiples — bracketing the current ~$16–19 trading range and explaining the market's deep ambivalence about this name.
